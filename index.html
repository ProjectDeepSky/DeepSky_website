The Problem

A $966 Billion Crisis
Hiding in Plain Sight

Clinical genomics interprets every patient's variants against a single reference genome — assuming one variant has one effect, regardless of genetic background.

$966B $966B
Annual rare disease burden

The reference genome is a map of nowhere.

Today's variant interpretation tools compare mutations to a single consensus sequence — a composite that represents no living individual. When a clinician asks "is this variant pathogenic?", the answer depends entirely on which genome it appears in. Current tools cannot account for this.

The result: diagnostic odysseys averaging 5–7 years, millions of variants classified as "uncertain significance," and a healthcare system flying blind at the molecular level.

Baby KJ was born with CPS1 deficiency — a devastating metabolic disorder. She received a personalized CRISPR therapy designed for her specific mutation. It required unprecedented coordination across multiple institutions and months of bespoke work.
The case that sparked DeepSky

Baby KJ's story is a triumph of modern medicine — and a warning. The level of coordination required to design her therapy cannot scale. For every Baby KJ who receives personalized treatment, thousands of patients wait with variants that current tools classify as "uncertain significance."

DeepSky was founded on a simple conviction: the technology to interpret every patient's genome in its full context should not be a one-off miracle. It should be infrastructure.

This isn't incremental improvement. It's a fundamental rethinking of how we interpret genetic variation — replacing the static encyclopedia with a living, personalized computation for every patient.
